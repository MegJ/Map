Whooping Cranes Are Migrating South Later Than Ever Before.

December 9, 2021

Migrating cranes fly against a cloudy sky. Photo by Chris Briggs

by Megan Rochlin

Every Fall, Whooping Cranes migrate over 1200 miles from their nesting grounds in Northern Canada to their winter homes in Southern Texas. Enthusiastic birders eagerly await the spectacle of the birds' migration as they cut across the middle of the United States. But the birds are not where they should be.

This year the Whooping Cranes are expected to be over two weeks late.

Through visualizing sightings of the crane from over 50 years of data collected by Cornell Ornithology, we can see the shifting migration patterns.

The map above shows that before the year 2000, most Whooping Cranes were already in their wintering grounds by the end of October. That is mostly true between 2000 and 2010 as well. However, after 2010, the map shows that most Whooping Cranes were still in Canada at the end of October.

“Anybody who works with Whooping Cranes in the Central Flyway knows that their migration has changed” Joel Jorgensen, a biologist with the Nebraska Game and Parks Commission, said in an interview with Audubon.
